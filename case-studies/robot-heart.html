01 — Context

The Mythology of the Bus

Robot Heart's 1972 Leyland double-decker bus was never designed to be a stage. It was purchased by founder George Mueller — a lighting pioneer who co-invented the LED systems on the Empire State Building — and driven into Black Rock City in 2008 with a handmade sound system bolted to its roof. When Keisuke joined as Lead Visual Artist in 2011, the bus had already earned a mythology on the playa: the place where sunrise happened, where 10,000 people stood in the dust as the light broke.

02 — Visual System

Designing for Transcendence

At 10,000 capacity with a 70,000-watt sound system rattling the desert floor, the visual work had to match the scale without competing with it. The challenge was creating a live visual language that felt organic to the playa — reading the DJ's arc, the crowd's energy, and the quality of the light as dawn approached. Not spectacle. Presence. LED arrays, generative canvas animations, and real-time VJ programming built into a system that could respond in the moment, shaping the emotional topology of a six-hour sunrise set.

03 — Further Future

Extending the Language Beyond the Playa

In 2015 and 2016, the Robot Heart collective organized Further Future — a luxury desert festival on the Moapa River Indian Reservation near Las Vegas. The visual identity had to carry the same emotional register as Burning Man but adapt to a different context: curated attendance, architectural stages, structured programming. The visual system was rebuilt for permanent structures rather than an art car, while preserving the essential quality that made Robot Heart's sunrises irreplaceable: the sense that the light, the sound, and the visuals were one continuous thing.

04 — Philosophy

The Thread

DJs like Diplo, Above & Beyond, Lee Burridge, and Rüfüs Du Sol have described Robot Heart as the most sought-after place to play on the playa — not because of the equipment, but because of what the environment allows. The visual work was always in service of that. A visual system at scale is most powerful when it disappears into the experience — when the crowd stops noticing the screen and starts feeling the light as part of something larger than a stage. That philosophy runs through every project in this portfolio, from the Gundam Metaverse's world logic to the Kunichika archive's color system. Honour what already exists before you design around it.
