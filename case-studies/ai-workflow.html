Opening Context

The Boundaries Are Getting Blurry

The lines between product manager, UX designer, and software engineer have always been somewhat artificial. The best builders I've worked with over 20+ years were never purely one thing. But the workflow infrastructure enforced the silos anyway: requirements documents went one direction, design files went another, code reviews happened somewhere else entirely. Communication was batched, one-way, and filtered through artifacts that represented decisions rather than enabling conversations.

I spent those 20 years doing exactly what a PM/UX person is supposed to do: writing market analysis, preparing user stories, building wireframes, crafting clickable prototypes — all of it designed to communicate design intent to engineers in a form they could act on. The craft was real. But the communication was still indirect. A developer interpreting a prototype. A QA engineer interpreting a spec. Each step introduced drift between what I intended and what shipped.

What's changing now isn't the importance of that craft. It's the channel. When the design can become a working proof-of-concept in hours rather than weeks, the conversation changes entirely. You're not explaining what you meant. You're showing it, running, and asking what needs to change.

The most important workflow design today isn't about tools. It's about how we communicate — openly, transparently, without the silos that filtered intent out of every handoff.

Figma MCP Server is the bridge that turns a design file into structured data an AI can act on directly. Design tokens, component hierarchies, image assets — extracted programmatically in minutes, not hours of redlining. The output isn't a PDF annotation for a developer to interpret. It's structured specification that removes the first translation step entirely.

Claude Code is where design intent becomes code. When I pass a token set and component hierarchy from Figma MCP, it doesn't generate boilerplate — it reads the relationship between spacing tokens and layout intent, between color roles and interaction states. Refinement happens in conversation. Not in JIRA tickets sent across a wall.

Google Antigravity — Google's cross-platform agentic IDE, launched November 2025 — handles multi-platform scaffolding with Gemini model support alongside Claude. It completes the stack: MCP extracts the design language, Antigravity builds the application structure, Claude Code fills in interaction logic and component detail.

The pipeline in practice: Figma design → MCP extraction (minutes) → Antigravity scaffolding (minutes) → Claude Code component build (hours) → side-by-side refinement (minutes). What the traditional workflow delivers in two to four weeks, this delivers the same day. The designer stays in the conversation from intent to shipped code — no handoff, no drift.

Impact

The Artifact Is the Conversation

The most specific outcome I can point to: a client-approved functional prototype, built from a high-fidelity Figma design, delivered the same day. No engineering sprint. No QA cycle. The client reviewed working code, not a mockup. They approved it on the first review. That conversation — where both sides are looking at the same running software — is qualitatively different from any conversation you can have over a static prototype. It's not "does this look right?" It's "does this work?"

You're looking at a second outcome right now. This portfolio — its design system, its case study templates, its interactive components — was built entirely through this pipeline. The design existed in Figma. The code was generated and iterated through Claude Code. The gap between what I designed and what you're reading is zero.

Reflection

The Role Isn't Changing. The Conversation Is.

After 20+ years as a PM and UX person, what strikes me most about this workflow isn't the speed — it's what it changes about who can be in the room. The traditional pipeline created expertise silos by necessity: you needed a developer to build anything testable, a QA engineer to validate it, a PM to manage the translation across those boundaries. The boundaries weren't just organizational. They were built into the tools.

What I'm experiencing now is the biggest personal shift in how I work: I can bring a proof-of-concept into a client meeting instead of a mockup. I can show a working prototype to a stakeholder who doesn't read Figma files. I can make a change during a conversation and show the result before the meeting ends. The conversation is different when both sides are looking at the same running software — more honest, more specific, more productive.

The craft of PM and UX — deep user research, rigorous requirement analysis, clear systems thinking — doesn't change. If anything it matters more, because the distance between a decision and its implementation is now hours, not weeks. Bad decisions ship faster. Good decisions ship better. The judgment still lives with the person doing the work. The workflow just removes the friction that used to slow it down.

From guidelines to tokens to AI-mediated implementation. Each career phase reduced the distance between design intent and shipped product. This workflow eliminates it — and opens the conversation to everyone in the room.
