When Slalom Build opened its Tokyo office, the opportunity was unique: heritage enterprises with 70–400 years of history facing digital transformation. As Director of Experience Design, I oversaw UX strategy across the entire practice, setting design vision for five enterprise clients spanning AI/ML, autonomous systems, and organizational transformation.

JRCS — From Waterfall to Autonomy

JRCS · Autonomous Vessel Decision Support System

JRCS, a 70-year-old marine hardware manufacturer, needed a computer vision product to detect objects at sea — addressing the shortage of ship captains. I led UX design for "command," requiring peripheral, ambient interactions for users who cannot look away from the ocean.

We delivered a working decision support product in nine months, transitioning JRCS from waterfall to agile development with a Microsoft partnership on mixed reality and edge computing. Their roadmap targets full autonomous multi-vessel control by 2030.

The hardest design problem wasn't the AI — it was earning the trust of captains who've navigated by instinct for thirty years. You don't replace intuition with a dashboard. You extend it.

Kawasaki Heavy Industries — Intelligence on the Rails

Kawasaki partnered with Slalom Build to create an intelligent track maintenance platform using computer vision and AI. The "Fastener" system inspects railway infrastructure, while the "Maintenance Advisor" optimizes repair routes and reduces disruption.

Kawasaki grew from zero software engineers to a team of 12–15 using agile methodology, with the platform field-tested by Union Pacific and BNSF Railway. The partnership transformed both the product and the organization.

When a century-old heavy manufacturer builds its first software team, the design challenge isn't the interface — it's the organisation. You're designing a new way of working, not just a new product.

Takenaka Corporation — 400 Years of Building, Reimagined

Takenaka Corporation · Founded 1610 · Agile Transformation

Takenaka Corporation, founded in 1610, built iconic Japanese structures including Tokyo Tower, Narita Airport, and Tokyo Dome. An aging workforce and productivity stagnation made digital transformation essential.

Slalom Build helped Takenaka adopt Agile methodology—a profound shift for a 400-year-old company. We built a digital construction platform on AWS with AI, IoT, and digital twin capabilities, enabling the company to iterate in two-week sprints.

Bandai Namco — 45 Years of IP Become a World

SIDE-G · Gundam Metaverse Virtual Space Colony

Bandai Namco invested $130M in SIDE-G, a virtual world where 45 years of Gundam heritage becomes a space fans inhabit. I defined UX strategy that makes fans feel like pilots, not visitors, using fan mental models and existing loyalties as the foundation for information architecture.

The avatar system earned affiliation markers and access rights through engagement, mirroring Gundam's hero arcs. Multiple entry points accommodate different fan types while maintaining continuity across anime, manga, games, and concerts.

An IP-native virtual world doesn't start with technology — it starts with the mythology fans already carry. Design your world to honour what they already believe.

CTC (Itochu Techno-Solutions) — The Multiplier Effect

CTC, Japan's leading IT services company, sent seven engineers to the Seattle Build Center to learn Slalom's Product Engineering Methodology. We built a real-time translation application on AWS, but the real delivery was knowledge transfer: CTC's engineers experienced agile product development firsthand.

Upon returning to Tokyo, CTC established their own Build Center service, extending Slalom's methodology across Japan. The capability transfer multiplied impact beyond the original project.

Building the Practice

Slalom Build opened the Tokyo office in late 2022 to bring cutting-edge product engineering to a market where methodology had stagnated. The international team from seven countries shared a commitment to agile practices and organizational transformation.

Each engagement transformed heritage companies' domain expertise into software. The design challenge went beyond interfaces—it was helping century-old organizations learn to iterate, ship in increments, and embrace uncertainty. The frontier was culture, not technology.
