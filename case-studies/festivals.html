Robot Heart

Robot Heart is a rolling art stage at Burning Man — no sponsors, no pre-rendered video, no corporate dressing. I built and ran its real-time visual engine: a system that listened and responded to music directly, where every visual output was a live computation triggered by the audio signal itself.

Image Placeholder
Black Rock City · Aerial · The Desert Stage

Building the Engine

TouchDesigner handled generative and audio-reactive graphics; Resolume managed VJ mixing and output routing to LED panels. Together, they formed a system designed for desert conditions with zero fallback — dust, heat, and no recovery time.

Music and light aren't two things. They're one thing — and I was engineering both ends of the signal.

Audio-Reactive Systems

I built beyond simple amplitude mapping: BPM detection synchronized visual timing to the musical pulse; frequency analysis mapped bass to geometric transformations, mids to color temperature, highs to particle systems. The result was a visual system that participated in the music, not illustrated it — light and sound arrived simultaneously.

The Main Stages

I brought these systems to EDC Las Vegas (100,000+ attendees per night), Ultra Music Festival in Miami (50,000+ per day), Detroit Movement, and Further Future. Each venue demanded reliable, real-time performance at scale without recovery time.

Image Placeholder
Robot Heart · Black Rock Desert → Main Stage · 100K+ Scale

Producing visuals for 100,000 people with zero latency teaches what screen-based practice cannot: latency is felt before it's perceived, and the feedback loop is zero-tolerance.

There are no bug fixes at a live festival. The system works or it doesn't, in front of 100,000 people.
