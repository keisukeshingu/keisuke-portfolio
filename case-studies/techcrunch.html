Context

The Conference That Needed to Look Like Its Own Future

TechCrunch Disrupt is the flagship event for the global startup and venture capital ecosystem — three days at Moscone Center in San Francisco where product launches, billion-dollar funding announcements, and Startup Battlefield finals compete for the same audience attention. An audience of 10,000+ people who read visual quality as a proxy for the quality of the ideas on stage.

In 2016, I was brought in alongside Kyle Gordon of Studio ZIRO to create the visual identity for the full event — every stage, every keynote, every branded moment across the three-day run. The brief was precise and nearly impossible: build a visual system that looks as forward-thinking as the companies presenting inside it.

The Work

San Francisco as Data

The visual concept started from a question: what does the city itself look like when you reduce it to pure information? Using real geographic map data and height-map elevation models of San Francisco, Kyle and I built a physically accurate, minimalist 3D rendering of the city — every block, every gradient of elevation from the waterfront to Twin Peaks, expressed as clean wireframe geometry. The city's actual structure became the visual system.

Multiple camera animations were developed — aerial pull-outs over the Embarcadero, tracking shots through SoMa at street level, slow reveals of the Bay Bridge geometry. Each was designed to feel like data visualization made cinematic: specific to the geography, abstract enough to read as motion-graphic identity rather than documentary footage. TechCrunch's color palette and type system were layered in at the compositing stage, fusing the brand into the geometry rather than sitting on top of it.

The delivery system was modular by design. Different camera cuts and timing variations could be assembled for different session types — keynote openers, speaker transitions, stage backgrounds, ambient holds — without requiring re-renders. The production team had a library of components that maintained visual consistency across three full days of programming.

A conference visual system works when it disappears — when the audience stops noticing the screen and starts focusing on the idea. The city render was the stage. The speakers were the story.

Impact

Three Days, Every Screen

The visual system ran without failure across the full three-day event — every keynote, every stage transition, every ambient screen in the Moscone Center. The modular production approach meant the event team could compose new variations from the asset library in real time as programming shifted, without returning to the original 3D pipeline.

For an audience that includes founders, investors, and press who have seen thousands of conference visuals, building from real geographic data rather than generic motion graphics created a system that felt genuinely specific to this event, in this city, in this moment — not interchangeable with any other conference brand.

Reflection

Specificity as Craft

The principle behind the San Francisco data render is one that runs through the whole portfolio: the data that already exists is always more interesting than the data you invent. The city's actual block structure, elevation gradients, and waterfront geometry carry inherent meaning — geographic, cultural, historical. Using it as the visual foundation gives the work specificity that stock motion graphics can never have, no matter how sophisticated the technique.

This principle connects directly to how I approach AI interface design. A dashboard that surfaces what the system actually knows, in the form most appropriate to how users actually think, is always more powerful than one that organizes information around what's easy to display. The craft question is the same in both cases: start with the real structure of the thing, and let that structure become the visual logic.
