Miselu C.24

OS-Level Audio Engineering

A Bluetooth LE MIDI keyboard controller with a USPTO patent — reducing Android audio latency from 250ms to under 10ms and making real-time musical performance possible on Android for the first time.

Hero image — Miselu C.24 Bluetooth MIDI keyboard

Role

Director of Software Products

Timeline

2011–2013

Platform

iOS, Android, Bluetooth LE MIDI

USPTO Patent Bluetooth LE MIDI Android Audio Google I/O 2012 KORG Yamaha

The Miselu C.24 was a portable, wireless musical instrument that connected to smartphones and tablets via Bluetooth Low Energy. As Director of Software Products, I designed and built the software ecosystem that made the hardware perform — and solved a platform-level problem that no one else had cracked.

The stock Android audio stack in 2012 had approximately 250ms of latency — a quarter-second delay between pressing a key and hearing sound. For reference, anything above 10ms is perceptible to musicians and makes real-time performance impossible. I forked the Android audio pipeline, bypassing the high-level audio framework to communicate directly with the hardware audio layer. This required deep OS-level engineering — understanding the Linux kernel's audio subsystem, the Android HAL (Hardware Abstraction Layer), and the timing characteristics of specific audio chipsets.

Latency comparison — 250ms stock Android vs. sub-10ms Miselu pipeline

The result: sub-10ms latency on Android — a breakthrough that made the C.24 the first viable real-time MIDI controller for the Android platform. The C.24 and the Neiro music creation app were demonstrated at Google I/O 2012, showcasing the latency breakthrough to thousands of developers.

I am a named inventor on US Patent 9,165,476 — covering the wireless MIDI controller technology that enabled low-latency musical instrument communication over Bluetooth Low Energy. I also established partnerships with KORG and Yamaha, validating the technology and opening distribution channels in the professional music industry.

A USPTO patent, a Google I/O demo, and partnerships with KORG and Yamaha — from a startup that dared to fork Android's audio pipeline to make music happen in real time.
