Situation

Premium Audio, Legacy Experience

In 2017, Denon and Marantz sat at #5 in the premium home audio market. The products themselves were acclaimed for sound quality — audiophiles trusted the engineering. But the user experience told a different story. Product reviews averaged 3.5 stars, and UX was consistently cited as the primary weakness.

The root cause ran deeper than interface polish. Leadership wanted to compete directly with Sonos, Apple, Google, and Amazon in the smart home and wireless streaming space through the HEOS multi-room audio platform. But HEOS wasn't fully integrated with the core audio products. The streaming app felt disconnected from the receivers and amplifiers that were the brand's foundation.

The Technical Debt

The TV GUI had the visual quality of an 8-bit game. The underlying architecture used bitmap graphics with no structured file system — every string of text, every icon was a rasterized image baked at a fixed resolution.

Key Constraints

Extreme hardware limitations — Small embedded displays (1–3 inches) with limited color depth. Every pixel counts. Legacy platform migration — Shift from bitmap to vector had to happen without disrupting the active product pipeline. Apple certification — AirPlay 2 and HomeKit required strict HIG compliance. Multi-platform delivery — Embedded Linux, iOS, Android, and TV GUI are four fundamentally different rendering environments.

Action

From Research to Ecosystem Redesign

Research: Understanding the Problem Space

I approached research through mixed methods: support data analysis revealed setup and initial configuration as the top frustration driver. Product review mining showed sound quality scores of 4.5–5.0★ but overall ratings of 3.5★ — the delta between acoustic quality and user experience was the gap to close.

The critical insight: audiophiles and mainstream users didn't need different products — they needed different layers of the same experience. A well-designed progressive disclosure system serves both.

The Audiophile

Deep control, precision, customization. Access to every setting, signal path transparency. Clunky interface undermines the premium price.

The Mainstream User

Simplicity, "it just works." Easy setup, streaming access, voice control. Too complex for daily use, poor smart home integration.

The Platform Migration — Bitmap to Vector

The foundational design decision: replacing the entire bitmap-based rendering system with a Qt framework on embedded Linux, converting all graphics to SVG with font-embedding and database-driven content. This solved resolution independence, localization at scale, and established a design system foundation — all in one architectural move.

The Monotype Breakthrough

The migration created an unexpected challenge: no standard font licensing model existed for embedded devices. I became the point person negotiating with Monotype to establish an entirely new licensing framework — covering per-device pricing at 60+ SKU scale, CJK character sets, embedded ROM constraints, and firmware update rights.

The resulting model saved multi-million dollars compared to bitmap production. But the impact went further: the framework I established was subsequently adopted by Audi, Porsche, BMW, and Mercedes for automotive infotainment systems.

Voice-First Setup Flow

Support data revealed product setup as the single largest frustration source. I redesigned the flow around three principles: guided onboarding via companion app (the HEOS mobile app as primary setup interface), multi-assistant integration (Siri, Alexa, and Google Assistant in a unified sequence), and progressive complexity (default simplicity with expert depth on demand).

Apple Ecosystem Compliance

AirPlay 2 and HomeKit certification for 50+ products required strict Apple HIG compliance. I translated Apple's requirements into our design guidelines, created per-category compliance checklists, and validated each product before submission to Apple's certification program.

Design Thinking

Fix the Framework, Not the Screens

My approach wasn't to redesign individual product interfaces — it was to fix the underlying technical and design frameworks so that better UX became the default output. When the rendering system makes premium typography impossible, you don't iterate on bitmap fonts — you replace the rendering system.

Problem Surface Fix Framework Fix
Ugly TV GUI Redesign bitmaps Migrate to Qt/SVG/font-embedding
Localization bottleneck Hire more producers Database-driven + Monotype licensing
Inconsistent UX Individual product specs Brand guidelines that scale
Complex setup Better manuals Voice-first, app-guided architecture

Constrained Hardware as Design Discipline

On a 1–3 inch display with physical buttons, you cannot fall back on visual complexity. Every decision must answer: what does the user need to know right now, and how do I show it in the fewest pixels possible? This constraint-driven rigor produces thinking that applies far beyond embedded systems.

What I'd Do Differently

Invest in a true component library earlier. The guidelines approach worked at scale but relied on each team's interpretation. Push for direct user testing sooner. Analytics identified the right problems, but observing users with prototypes would have shortened iteration. Document the framework migration methodology — it was a repeatable process valuable to the broader industry.

Connecting to the Bigger Picture

Platform thinking over feature thinking — designing for 60+ SKUs across four platforms forces systems thinking directly applicable to large-scale design at Apple, Meta, or Google. Hardware-software bridge — the ability to design across embedded firmware, mobile apps, and TV interfaces spans the full stack. Cross-industry influence — the Monotype licensing model I established became an industry standard adopted by premium automotive brands.
