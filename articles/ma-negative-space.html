Ma — The Power of Negative Space in Digital Interfaces

Keisuke Shingu·January 15, 2026·8 min read

The principle of ma (間): emptiness as active design — space that frames presence and invites perception.

On This Page

  1. The Western Bias Toward Filling
  2. Ma as Design Principle
  3. Information Density vs. Clarity
  4. Applying Ma to Enterprise Dashboards
  5. The Courage to Leave Empty

Walk into any enterprise software application, and you'll witness the same pattern: every pixel is occupied. Dashboard tiles stretch edge-to-edge. Lists scroll endlessly. Modals overlay modals. Charts compress data points until they become visual noise. This isn't accident. It's the inheritance of a Western design philosophy that equates fullness with value, density with functionality, and visible features with user satisfaction.

But something happens when you step into a Japanese garden. Or look at a Noh theater stage. Or listen to a Miles Davis solo. The power doesn't live in what is present — it lives in what is absent. The space between the notes. The emptiness that makes the form legible. The pause that gives weight to what comes next.

This is ma (間). And it might be the most underutilized design principle in digital product development.

The Western Bias Toward Filling

There's a phrase I heard constantly during my time at Denon/Marantz: "We're not using the space." This came from product managers, executives, and (let's be honest) designers influenced by the same scarcity mindset. Every interaction surface was an opportunity. Every empty corner, a missed feature.

This bias runs deep. In the early days of software, screen real estate was expensive. Limited RAM meant every visible element had weight. So we learned to optimize for density. That lesson embedded itself into our DNA. Even now — when screens are infinite and memory is abundant — we fill spaces out of habit, not necessity.

The "more features = more value" equation is particularly pernicious in enterprise software. Stakeholders see competing products and ask: "But does ours have that?" The answer is often: "No, and we have seventeen other things instead." But without white space to frame each feature, all seventeen become visual clutter. The dashboard doesn't look feature-rich; it looks panicked.

This is where ma enters the conversation. But not as "white space" in the Anglo-Saxon sense of "empty space left over after design is done." Ma is intentional. Pregnant. Active.

Ma as Design Principle

The Japanese concept of ma (間) translates poorly into English. "Space" or "negative space" misses the point entirely. Ma is the interval, the gap, the distance that enables perception. It's the silence between drumbeats that makes rhythm possible. The white wall in a gallery that frames the single painting. The pause in conversation that invites response.

In traditional Japanese arts, ma is never empty. It's full of potential. A tea master leaves the stage bare not because they've run out of props, but because the emptiness invites the viewer's imagination to complete the scene. An ikebana arrangement uses more space than flowers — the negative space is the design.

The architect Tadao Ando demonstrates this principle in concrete forms. His walls are austere, almost severe. But that severity creates a container — for light, for shadow, for thought. You don't notice how empty the space is; you notice how your own presence fills it.

In interface design, ma serves a similar function. It's not the absence of information. It's the presence of breathing room around information. It's the design choice to let an element stand alone rather than compete with neighbors. It's the courage to say: "This card deserves this much space because what it communicates is worth the real estate."

Information Density vs. Clarity

There's a persistent myth in product development: that information density and information clarity are the same thing. They're not. In fact, they're often inverse.

Cognitive science backs this up. When Nielsen Norman conducted their studies on web readability, they found that white space increased comprehension by approximately 20%. A dense page of text is harder to scan. A text with generous line-spacing and margins is easier to parse. The eye knows where to go. The brain knows what to prioritize.

Apple understood this principle before it became fashionable. Their product pages aren't dense — they're sparse. A single image. A short headline. Lots of white space. Yet every Apple product page communicates more than pages crammed with bullet points and feature lists. Why? Because the space itself is communicative. It says: "This is important enough to stand alone."

In dashboard design, the effect is even more pronounced. A metrics card surrounded by generous padding performs better in user testing than the same card in a tight grid. Users look at it longer. They're more likely to act on the information. The ma — the breathing room — actually increases engagement with the data.

This seems counterintuitive to stakeholders. "We're paying for this screen space," they say. "We should use all of it." But research shows the opposite: users trust interfaces that don't try to cram everything in. There's an implicit signal: "We respect your attention too much to waste it."

The space around a data point tells the user as much as the data point itself — it says: this matters enough to stand alone.

Applying Ma to Enterprise Dashboards

The most tangible example of ma in my own work came during the HEOS app redesign at Denon/Marantz. The original dashboard packed six metrics tiles into a 4-column grid, with minimal spacing. Dense, efficient, and almost impossible to parse at a glance.

Our redesign applied ma principles: fewer cards per row, generous padding around each card, increased line-height in text, clear visual hierarchy through spacing rather than visual weight. The result was a dashboard that looked less full but communicated more.

In user testing, engagement metrics improved. Users spent more time with individual cards. Error rates dropped. Feature discoverability increased — not because we added more features, but because the space around each feature made them legible.

There's a technique I've since applied to other projects called the "negative space audit." For every interface element, ask: How much space is required for this to be understood? Not: How much space can we afford? The question shifts from scarcity to intention.

In the HEOS app, this meant:

  • Metric cards: Increased padding from 8px to 16px on all sides. The card itself took up less of the viewport, but communicated more clearly.
  • Section spacing: Between functional areas of the dashboard, we doubled the whitespace. Users could now group related cards mentally without visual guides.
  • Typography hierarchy: Rather than making headings bold or colored, we gave them more space. The space is the emphasis.
  • Action buttons: Instead of squeezing buttons into compact toolbars, we let them breathe. A solitary button in white space is more usable than three buttons in a 32px bar.

The pushback from stakeholders was predictable: "We're wasting space." But the data contradicted them. Dashboards with generous spacing saw higher adoption, fewer support tickets, and more feature usage. The paradox: by using less of the screen, we made it more valuable.

Ma is not the absence of design. It is the presence of intention in what you choose not to show.

The Courage to Leave Empty

The hardest part of designing with ma isn't understanding the principle. It's defending it in a room full of stakeholders who see empty space as waste.

"Our competitors have fifteen features here," someone will say, pointing at a wireframe. "Why don't we?" The answer — "Because those fifteen features are harder to use because they're crammed together" — rarely lands well in the moment.

But here's what I've learned: ma works best when it's defended as design intent, not as accident. You have to be prepared to say: "This space is intentional. We tested with and without it. The version with ma performed better." Data is the only language stakeholders trust when white space is at stake.

There's also a cultural component. In the West, we're conditioned to fill space. In our visual culture, emptiness is often associated with poverty or incompleteness. ("We couldn't afford a full page of content, so here's white space.") Japanese aesthetics, by contrast, celebrate emptiness as abundance. The empty space is content.

Bringing this perspective into Western product teams requires patience. But once you see it working — once you watch users engage more deeply with an interface that respects their attention — it becomes impossible to go back.

The future of digital interface design isn't about fitting more in. It's about respecting the space between what we show. It's about understanding that negative space isn't a failure of design; it's the presence of intention.
