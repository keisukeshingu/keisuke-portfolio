Design Tokens Are Not Enough — Building Living Systems

Keisuke Shingu·December 8, 2025·10 min read

A design system architecture diagram showing the layers between tokens, components, patterns, and organizational adoption.

Every design system pitch starts the same way: "We'll define tokens — colors, spacing, typography — and everything will be consistent." It's a compelling promise. Define once, use everywhere. Change a hex value, watch it ripple across fifty screens. The allure of tokens is the allure of control.

But after building and maintaining design systems across publishing platforms serving Forbes and Condé Nast, premium audio ecosystems spanning 60+ products, and multi-agent AI platforms — I've learned that tokens are necessary but not sufficient on their own. They solve the easiest 20% of the consistency problem and leave the hardest 80% untouched.

The Token Promise

Design tokens are brilliant in their simplicity. A semantic layer that maps intent to value: --color-primary rather than #1D1D1F. They abstract visual decisions from implementation, enabling theme switching, dark mode, and brand customization without touching component code.

The token specification has matured significantly. The W3C Design Tokens Community Group has standardized the format. Figma, Style Dictionary, and a growing ecosystem of tools can generate, transform, and distribute tokens across platforms. The infrastructure is there.

So why do design systems still fracture?

Where Systems Break

Systems don't break at the token level. They break at the seams — the places where components meet, where patterns compose, where edge cases accumulate, and where organizational incentives diverge from system goals.

Design systems don't fail because the tokens are wrong. They fail because nobody designed the rules for how tokens combine, when patterns override components, and who arbitrates conflicts.

In a premium audio design system spanning 60+ products, the tokens were precise — every shade of the interface specified. But when a multi-room audio team needed a "now playing" state that combined background blur, album art tinting, and playback controls, the tokens couldn't express the relationship between these elements. The system had vocabulary but no grammar.

The same pattern repeated at the AI platform. Tokens handled color and spacing beautifully. But when we needed to express "this agent's confidence is declining over time, and the visual treatment should reflect both the current state and the trajectory," we were beyond what tokens could express. We needed design logic, not design values.

Living Architecture

The metaphor that helped us was architecture — not software architecture, but physical architecture. A building isn't defined by its materials (tokens). It's defined by its structural system (how loads transfer), its spatial program (how rooms relate), and its response to context (climate, site, culture).

A living design system needs three things beyond tokens: composition rules that define how components combine; behavioral patterns that define how states, transitions, and interactions work; and governance structures that define who can extend the system and how.

Three Layers Deep

Layer 1: Semantic Tokens (the vocabulary)

This is where most systems stop. Color, typography, spacing, elevation, motion timing. Essential but insufficient.

Layer 2: Composition Patterns (the grammar)

Rules for how tokens combine in context. A card component in a grid has different spacing rules than the same card in a sidebar. A button's visual weight changes based on its container's information density. These aren't token values — they're relationships between values that shift based on context.

Layer 3: Behavioral Contracts (the rhetoric)

Agreements about how the system responds to state changes, error conditions, loading states, and edge cases. When an agent reports low confidence, every component in the decision interface should respond in a coordinated way — not just change color, but adjust information density, surface alternative actions, and modify the interaction pattern.

Tokens give you consistency of appearance. Composition patterns give you consistency of experience. Behavioral contracts give you consistency of meaning.

Organizational Reality

The hardest part of a design system isn't technical — it's organizational. Systems succeed or fail based on governance: who decides when to add a new pattern, how exceptions are handled, and what happens when a product team's deadline conflicts with system integrity.

The systems that survive are the ones that acknowledge this reality from the start. They build contribution models that make it easier to extend the system than to work around it. They create decision frameworks for when to customize versus when to standardize. They invest in documentation that explains not just what the system does, but why it works that way.

In a publishing platform that served 95% of the print industry, this lesson came through years of maintaining a design system that thousands of designers depended on daily. The system's longevity came not from its technical sophistication but from its governance model — clear contribution paths, transparent decision-making, and a deep respect for the practitioners who used it.

Design tokens are a foundation. But a foundation isn't a building. The system that will serve your organization for years — that will evolve gracefully across product launches, team changes, and platform migrations — needs grammar, rhetoric, and governance on top of vocabulary. Start with tokens. But don't stop there.
